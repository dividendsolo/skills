# Investment Checklist

The concrete go/no-go screening questions the `checklist` skill runs every investment idea through. Where `/munger` asks "which mental models pop?", this asks "does the idea clear each gate, and can I say *why*?". The two are complements: run the lattice for insight, run this for a disciplined screen.

**This is a living document.** It is seeded with a canonical starting set below. Add, cut, and reword freely so it becomes *your* checklist, not a textbook's. Keep each item one line: **a question worded to demand a reasoned answer, not a yes/no.** Terse enough that the whole screen fits in working memory during a run.

A question is only "passed" when there is a written reason behind it. "Yes" with no "why" is a fail.

---

## 1. Understand it (circle of competence)
- **Do I actually understand how this makes money?** Could I explain the business to someone in two minutes without hand-waving?
- **Is this inside my circle of competence,** or am I stretching to make it fit?
- **What would I have to believe** for this to work, stated as a falsifiable thesis?

## 2. Quality and durability
- **Will it be a better business in 5 years? Why?** (force an explicit durability/compounding reason, not a yes/no.)
- **What is the moat,** specifically: brand, switching costs, network effects, scale, regulation? Which one, how deep?
- **Is the moat widening or eroding,** and what is the evidence in the numbers?
- **What kills this business?** Name the most likely cause of permanent decline.
- **Is there a structural tailwind** doing real work, or is it swimming upstream?

## 3. Management and capital allocation
- **How do they allocate capital** (reinvest, buy back, pay down, acquire, dividend), and has it created value?
- **Are incentives and ownership** aligned with long-term per-share value?
- **Are they honest and candid** in good and bad years? Does the reporting hide or reveal?

## 4. Financials and balance sheet
- **Does the cash flow back the story** (real free cash flow, not just accounting earnings)?
- **Returns on capital:** are they high and sustainable, or competed away?
- **Can the balance sheet survive a bad year?** Debt, maturities, covenants, liquidity.
- **Where is this in its cycle,** and am I underwriting peak earnings as if they were normal?

## 5. Valuation and margin of safety
- **What does the price assume,** and is that optimism or pessimism?
- **How wrong can I be** on growth/margins and still not lose money (margin of safety)?
- **Is the downside capped and the upside open,** or the reverse (asymmetry)?
- **What is the realistic 5-year return,** and from what (multiple, growth, yield)?

## 6. Risks and pre-mortem (invert)
- **It's 5 years from now and this was a mistake: what happened?** Write the bear case the thesis is glossing over.
- **What single point of failure** would impair this permanently (customer, supplier, regulation, key person, leverage)?
- **Which of my own biases** is loudest here: commitment, social proof, authority/halo, FOMO, confirmation? (cross-check `/munger` psychology list.)

## 7. Fit and opportunity cost
- **Is this better than what I already own** or my best current alternative (not better than zero)?
- **Does the position size** respect how sure I actually am and how much I could lose?
- **Is this just too hard?** If it needs a convoluted story to work, does it belong on the too-hard pile?

---

## Disposition
After running the gates, land on one, with reasons:
- **Go** - clears the gates; name the two or three things that most need to stay true.
- **No-go** - fails a gate that matters; name which and why.
- **Needs work** - promising but a key gate is unanswered; name what to investigate next.

Never a buy/sell directive. The output is reasoning and a disposition. **Not financial advice.**
