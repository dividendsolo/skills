# Cyclicals Playbook

Reference for the `cyclicals` skill. A living list - grow it as you refine the tells. Keep entries terse.

## What counts as a cyclical
- Makes or sells **expensive or deferrable** goods - customers postpone the purchase when cash is short. (Lynch's contrast: deodorant, dental floss, Big Macs, drugstores are NOT cyclical - people buy them in any economy.)
- **Earnings-line test:** a growth stock's earnings slope steadily up (Merck); a cyclical's wobble up and down (Alcoa). A picture is worth a thousand numbers.
- **Classic sectors:** steel, aluminum and metals, autos, machinery and capital goods, homebuilders, appliances, furniture, airlines, shipping, chemicals, paper/containerboard, semiconductors, hotels, high-end restaurants, advertising. Some hide in plain sight (IBM became a cyclical once mainframes dominated; waste management is cyclical - less waste when business is slow).

## Cycle position - what to read
- Demand trend; capacity utilization; order book / backlog.
- Inventories: building = late-cycle; cleared/lean = turning up.
- Pricing power and gross margins vs the cycle's OWN history (not vs other businesses).
- Capex: boom and new capacity = top; cuts, idling, bankruptcies = bottom.
- Balance sheet and dividend: cuts cluster near troughs.
- Sentiment and the macro backdrop.

## Normalized earnings
- Estimate **mid-cycle EPS**: average through a full peak-to-trough cycle, or apply a normal margin to normal volume.
- State explicitly whether current earnings are near peak or trough.
- Never extrapolate peak or trough earnings as permanent.
- Watch for structural change: a "normal" cycle assumes the industry's economics have not permanently shifted (new low-cost supply, demand destruction, technology change).

## The P/E inversion (the central cyclical trap)
- **Low P/E on record/peak earnings = expensive and late.** The multiple is low precisely because the market expects earnings to fall. Classic value trap.
- **High or N/A P/E on depressed/trough earnings = potentially cheap and early.**
- So a cyclical often screens "cheapest" exactly when it is most dangerous, and "expensive" exactly when it is most opportune. Always value on the normalized number.

## Top tells (near peak - caution)
- Low P/E on record earnings; record margins.
- Universally touted; "supercycle" / "this time it's structural" talk.
- Capacity expansion, capex boom, new entrants pouring in.
- Inventories building; analyst upgrades piling up.

## Bottom tells (near trough - opportunity)
- Losses or no earnings (P/E high or N/A); dividends cut.
- Bleakest sentiment; "this one is different" / depression talk.
- Capacity shut, capex slashed, bankruptcies and consolidation.
- The consumer starting to lead a recovery (cars, homes, appliances), which then revives businesses up the chain (lumber, steel, capital goods, advertisers, the media that sells them space).

## Lynch's framing (attributed, from the source piece)
- The best time to get involved is when the economy is weakest, earnings are lowest, and public sentiment is bleakest. (He loaded up on cyclicals in the 1981-82 slump - a key to Magellan's record.)
- S&P's "The Outlook" studied the 8 post-WWII recessions: in every case the cyclical groups gained 50% or more in the five months after the market bottomed, more than double the advance of the S&P 500.
- Do NOT own cyclicals in a depression - but every recession brings out skeptics predicting one. The discipline is telling a normal cycle apart from a genuine structural break.
- Buying early in a recovery is never emotionally easy. That difficulty is the point: the bleakness is the signal.
